<section class="section">
    <h2>What is Artificial Intelligence?</h2>
    <p>
        Artificial Intelligence refers to the simulation of human intelligence in machines that are 
        programmed to think and learn like humans. The term was first coined in 1956 at Dartmouth College.
    </p>
</section>

<section class="section">
    <h2>Key Concepts We Explored</h2>
    
    <div class="content-card">
        <h3>Machine Learning</h3>
        <p>
            A subset of AI where computers learn from data without being explicitly programmed. 
            Instead of following rigid instructions, ML algorithms identify patterns and make decisions 
            based on examples.
        </p>
        <p><strong>Real-world example:</strong> Email spam filters that learn to recognize spam based on millions of labeled emails.</p>
    </div>

    <div class="content-card">
        <h3>Neural Networks</h3>
        <p>
            Computing systems inspired by biological neural networks in the human brain. They consist of 
            interconnected nodes (neurons) that process information using connectionist approaches to computation.
        </p>
        <p><strong>Real-world example:</strong> Image recognition systems that can identify objects in photos with high accuracy.</p>
    </div>

    <div class="content-card">
        <h3>Large Language Models (LLMs)</h3>
        <p>
            AI models trained on vast amounts of text data that can understand and generate human language. 
            They use deep learning techniques to predict and produce coherent text responses.
        </p>
        <p><strong>Real-world example:</strong> Chatbots, content generators, and code completion tools.</p>
    </div>

    <div class="content-card">
        <h3>Generative AI</h3>
        <p>
            AI systems that can create new content, including text, images, music, or code, based on 
            patterns learned from training data.
        </p>
        <p><strong>Real-world example:</strong> AI image generators that create artwork from text descriptions.</p>
    </div>

    <div class="content-card">
        <h3>Topic Modeling</h3>
        <p>
            A text analysis technique that discovers abstract "topics" in a collection of documents. 
            It helps identify themes and patterns across large corpora of text.
        </p>
        <p><strong>Real-world example:</strong> Analyzing news articles to discover recurring themes over time.</p>
    </div>
</section>

<section class="section">
    <h2>Comparing Language Models</h2>
    <p>
        In our course, students used LM Studio to compare different open-source language models:
    </p>
    <ul>
        <li><strong>Model Sizes:</strong> Comparing responses from models of varying parameter counts</li>
        <li><strong>Different Origins:</strong> Testing models developed by different organizations</li>
        <li><strong>Identical Prompts:</strong> Observing how the same input produces different outputs</li>
    </ul>
    <div class="highlight-box">
        <p>
            <strong>Key Insight:</strong> These comparisons helped students understand that AI responses 
            are not fixed facts but depend heavily on how models are trained, their size, and their 
            specific design choices.
        </p>
    </div>
</section>
